Notes on accuracy

If a function is not listed below, consider it accurate.

nutate

Longitude and obliquity are accurate within 0".01 and after 1,000 years

calc_moon_pos

The algorithm this was adapted from in Chapter 47 of Meeus 1998 has an accuracy of 10" in the Moon's longitude and 4" in the Moon's latitude. In this adaptation, right ascension and declination are calculated instead, and they were intermediate products in finding the Moon's longitude and latitude. As a result, we can reasonably assume neither the right ascension nor the declination will inaccurate by a degree greater than 10".

calc_ha and calc_lst

The formulae used in Burnett 1998 make logical sense, but that does not necessarily mean they are accurate. Burnett did not provide accuracy figures for the relationships.

calc_moon_phase

The moon phase returned will be accurate within a few minutes of local time, according to Meeus 1998, Chapter 49.

check_eclipse

From my testing, a given date with a solar eclipse has a good (>90%) chance of being detected. A given date with a lunar eclipse is less predictable, with only 30-50% of lunar eclipse dates being detected in testing.

calc_illumination

According to Chapter 48 of Meeus 1998, the illuminated percentage will always be accurate within 0.14 percentage points.

calc_phase_angle

The formula provided by CASTOR in "Finding the Moon's Phase Angle" is believed to accurately reflect the relationship between progress in the lunar cycle and phase angle.

get_moon_img

The image returned by this function is not to scale as the Moon's phase function is neglected, meaning the actual appearance of the Moon will have a smoother visual transition between the illuminated and dark potions.

get_system_img

The following are not to scale: the sizes of the Sun, Earth, Moon, the radii of the Earth's orbit around the Sun and the Moon's orbit around the Earth, and the shapes of the orbits themselves (they should be elliptical). The relative positions of all three bodies is accurate.
